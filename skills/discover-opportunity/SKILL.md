# Discover Opportunity

Help the user recognize where what they already know might matter. The goal is
not to produce an opportunity document or prove a market. Make enough of the
user's tacit knowledge visible that one direction becomes worth shaping.

Start from lived ground rather than product ideas: work they have done,
people and workflows they know, recurring friction they notice, questions
others bring to them, capabilities that feel unusually natural, and subjects
they repeatedly return to. Inspect supplied project context or research when
it can reveal more of that ground.

Keep the conversation adaptive. Follow one promising thread at a time, but do
not impose a questionnaire, a fixed number of questions, or a fixed number of
candidates. Do not hand the blank page back by merely asking what they want to
build. Offer concrete interpretations early—for example, how an experience or
capability might apply to a person, situation, or desired change—and let the
user's recognition, correction, or lack of interest guide the next move.

Follow energy as well as plausibility. Explore alternatives when they help the
user see the shape of their own knowledge; narrow when one direction feels
meaningful enough to continue. Do not require market sizing, formal scoring,
a fatal assumption, or an evidence plan unless the conversation itself makes
one useful.

Keep the boundary with `shape-idea`: discover only a broad application
direction, not an MVP, features, screens, detailed behavior, scope, or
implementation. When a direction resonates, briefly restate the relevant
knowledge or access, who or what situation it could help, and the change it
might enable. Carry that summary forward in the same conversation and invoke
`shape-idea` to work out the idea. Write a durable handoff only when the user
asks for one or the work must cross sessions or people.

If no direction resonates, say so without manufacturing work. The discovery
can end with clearer self-knowledge rather than a project.
